Motivation.

This project brings together two things I care deeply about: understanding people and writing code.

Before I pivoted into Computer Science, I studied Sociology — a field that taught me to think critically about how ideas spread and how communities form around them. I’ve written essays on what feminism means today, the rise of anti-vaxxers, and how modern cults use online platforms to recruit and retain followers. What stood out to me across these topics was the power of misinformation — how a rumour can start small and yet influence entire networks of people, often with serious real-world effects.

That insight led to this simulation: a way to model how rumours might spread through a network of individuals, with different parameters influencing who gets "infected" by the rumour and how quickly.

I chose to build this in Java because it's a language I’m comfortable with, and one that’s particularly strong when it comes to building structured, scalable simulations. I also wanted to push myself beyond just scripting or visualising — I wanted to build something that reflected solid object-oriented thinking and made good use of classes, interfaces, and simulation loops.

This was more than a coding exercise. It was a way to connect my sociological background with my growing skills in software development — and to explore how tech can be used to better understand the social challenges we face.

How I Built It.

Language used: Java — great for modelling object-based systems.

Main Components.

  • Main.java: Entry point of the program, sets up the simulation.
  • Person.java: Each person has traits like whether they’ve heard or spread the rumour.
  • Network.java: Creates a social graph of people and hadles who is connected to whom.
  • RumourSimulation.java: Runs the simulation over time — deciding who hears the rumour and who spreads it.
  • Stats.java: Tracks and outputs data like how many people have heard the rumour and who spreads it.

The Logic.

  • The sumulation starts with one person hearing a rumour.
  • Each time step, people interact with their neighbours.
  • Depedning on the probability (e.g., how gullible or cautious they are), people decide whether to beleive or share the rumour
  • A proportion of people are fact-checkers — they independently verify that they hear. If they fact-check a rumour, they become immune to it and stop spreading it.
  • The rumour keeps spreading until it fizzles out or everyone has heard it.

What I Learned.

  • How to design and implement a simulation from scratch using object-orientated principles.
  • Deeper understanding of how rumours spread and the factors that can accelerate or suppress that spread — like social connections and gullibility.
  • Practiced breaking down a complex idea into modular, manageable Java classes and logic.
  • Balancing sociology ideas with technical modelling — a challenge I really enjoyed.
